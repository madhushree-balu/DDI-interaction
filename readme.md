Abstract
One of the primary reasons for drug reactions is the interaction of multiple drugs, which results in severe harm to patients, a rise in hospitalization rates, and unnecessary spending in the healthcare sector. The drug-drug interaction checking systems currently employed may only check two drugs at a time and employ a "one-size-fits-all" alerting system, in which the differences in patients are not taken into account, resulting in physicians ignoring most of the alerts. In this paper, an AI-powered personalized DDI risk assessment system is proposed, which provides patient-centric alerts with organ-level risk ratings. The system currently employs the DrugBank database of drug interactions, along with the individual laboratory values, medical history, and current medications of the patient, to offer personalized risk scores. This system, named “PolyGuard” (Personalized Polypharmacy Risk Score Prediction System), offers three perspectives through the analysis of interaction risk by organ systems, risk adjustment according to the health condition of each patient, and the analysis of combinations of several drugs taken together to detect risks that would not be detected by any pair of drugs alone. The aim of this system is to provide clinicians and patients with clear risk information such as the organ at risk, reasons for the risk, and what to monitor.
Keywords: Drug-Drug Interaction Adverse Drug Reactions Polypharmacy Risk Score Prediction Clinical Decision Support Personalized Medicine Natural Language Processing Multi-Organ Toxicity Pharmacovigilance Explainable AI.
 
I. Introduction
In a situation where a patient is using a combination of drugs at the same time, for example, in a situation referred to as polypharmacy, the risk of a toxic drug interaction is extremely high. This is especially true for the elderly and patients with chronic illnesses, meaning that they are likely to be using a combination of five, eight, or twelve drugs at the same time. This is a very serious situation, as in the United States alone, more than 100,000 deaths occur annually due to serious drug interactions that can be prevented, with 30 percent of hospitalizations among the elderly population (65+ years) being attributed to complications with drugs of the same kind, while the economic burden of preventable drug interactions is estimated to be over 177 billion per year [1][2].
The tools used by the physicians to alert them regarding the interactions of the drug are, on a basic level, outdated because they alert all people in the same way. If there are two drugs that interact, the system alerts the user regarding this as a major interaction, not to be taken, whether the patient is a young healthy person or an old weak person with kidney problems. These two patients are at two different levels of risk, and they are alerted about the same. It is no surprise that clinical research has shown that physicians do not respond to 90 percent of these alerts, not out of indifference, but due to the lack of specificity of the alert to take action [1][2].
This system is proposed as the solution to this problem. PolyGuard provides a personalized risk level for the patient, as opposed to generic yes/no notifications, based on the lab results, pre-existing medical conditions, and organ functions of the particular patient. It also determines which organ is most vulnerable, which drug combinations are toxic and which combinations will never be toxic, and why it does so in a manner that is easily understandable to the doctor, so that the doctor can utilize this information now. The implementation phase is the next step of this research.
II. Related Work
There are a few methods of predicting drug-drug interactions that have been investigated by researchers. Most of the recent literature is about using machine learning or deep learning on molecular structure data. To name a few, SSF-DDI [5] considers the chemical string and the structural data of a drug, which is helpful in detecting the interaction between two drugs. MDG-DDI [7] constructs a graph where the drugs are the nodes and the attributes are the edges, and then uses this graph to generate the predictions. The construction of a neural network is itself being automated by AutoDDI [14] to enhance the predictions. These systems are very impressive for their ability to predict the presence of an interaction, but they are not designed to answer the most crucial questions that are raised in the real-world setting: which organ is affected, what is the severity of this particular patient, and whether a cocktail of all the patient’s drugs is safe in general.
The gaps in real-world have been pointed out in other researches. Li et al. [8] studied real harm in terms of drug interactions for hospitalized patients and concluded that the existing approaches do not have clinically significant risks. Hagiwara et al. [10] have directly linked polypharmacy to the large number of adverse events among elderly patients. Carriero et al. [13] have argued that the AI system has to be explainable and, therefore, useful in the real-world setting, i.e., the physician has to know why the risk was pointed out, not that it was pointed out.
Critical Gap: There does not exist a clinical tool that integrates the risk information of the organs, individual adjustments for patients, and cumulative multi-drug toxicity into a single clinical tool. PolyGuard fills this gap.
III. Motivation and Problem Statement
The driving force behind the design of PolyGuard is based on three issues.
The first issue with the current systems is that they consider all patients equally. The same warning message about a drug interaction related to kidney stress is only relevant to a healthy 25-year-old individual and a patient with a failing kidney at the age of 70 years old, or at least this is what the current systems believe. The risk of the elderly patient could actually be ten times higher. A good system should consider the health status of each patient.
The second issue with the current systems is that they do not consider the use of their personal health data of the patient. Information such as the kidney function (which is calculated by eGFR), the liver enzyme levels (ALT, Ast), or the amount of blood platelets is always documented in the patient’s file but never used to adjust the risk scores in DDI. The PolyGuard system will be designed to use similar information to calculate risk scores that would be relevant to the individual.
Thirdly, the current systems are designed to check only the combinations of two drugs. If the patient is taking six different drugs, the system will check all possible combinations (Drug A + Drug B, Drug A + Drug C, and so on) but will never ask: What is the combined effect of all six drugs on, for example, the kidneys? They may all be evaluated as moderate risk but the combined kidney burden of all six drugs may be dangerous. The PolyGuard will be designed to check on the combined danger.
Conclusion to all the above, PolyGuard is an attempt to provide a solution for 5 questions, which cannot be answered by present solutions: What drug combinations are there? Which organ is in danger in each of the combinations? What effect does the health status of this patient have on the danger? Is there an overload of any organ by a combination of drugs? What is it that the doctor should do about it?
IV. Innovative Contributions
A. Multi-Organ Risk Decomposition.
Most DDI computer software uses one general level of severity for an interaction (Low, Moderate, High). However, PolyGuard asks the question: "What specific organ is being threatened?" The computer software uses a drug interaction to one or more of ten body systems; cardiovascular, renal (kidney), hepatic (liver), gastrointestinal, central nervous system, hematologic (blood), endocrine, musculoskeletal, respiratory, and immune.
This is done by the use of keywords. For example, if the drug interaction description uses the keyword "bleeding," the computer software assigns risk points to both the cardiovascular system and the blood (hematologic) system. If it uses the keyword "nephrotoxicity" (kidney damage), it is a point that is assigned to the renal system alone. If it says it will "prolong" (heart rhythm disturbance), it assigns points to the cardiovascular system. What is happening at the end is not the points, but rather a profile of how the organs are at risk - giving specific information, not a general guideline.
B. Vulnerability Adjustment in relation to patients.
PolyGuard changes the baseline risk score based on the individual health of the patient after the baseline risk score has been determined based on the data of the drug interaction. The diagnosis is determined based on 16 clinical data that have been previously measured as part and parcel of the clinical treatment: the age and weight of patients, the presence of certain conditions (kidney disease, liver diseases, heart failure, or diabetes), and laboratory results (eGFR, liver enzyme levels, blood platelets, and potassium).
The adjustment will be done by the multiplier. If the patient has less severe kidney function, i.e. eGFR of 42, which means moderate kidney disease, then the computer multiplies the score of the risk associated with the kidney by a factor of about 2. The score of the risk associated with the liver will be increased if the patient has a high level of liver enzyme. If the lab results of the patient are normal in all aspects, then the basic scores will be used as they are. This means that two patients with the same combination of drugs will have different scores of risk if their health condition will be different - which is exactly how clinical risk should be implemented.
C. Cascade Detection
Cascade detection is a response to the problem of pairwise checking, which is what would happen if there were many drugs that were all stressing the same organ at the same time. If the patient were taking six drugs with three individual scores of 3 each of low-to-moderate kidney risk, the pairwise checker would not report an alarming situation for any pair. However, if the three drugs were all simultaneously damaging the kidney, then the loss of effect could be serious.
PolyGuard calculates the sum of the risk scores for kidney damage for any two drugs. If the sum is above a certain level, it calls a "cascade alert," which is a reminder that the combination therapy is damaging to that organ, even though neither of the two pairs is. The patient's individual risk is factored in at this point, so that a patient with compromised kidneys will reach the danger threshold sooner than a normal patient on the same medications.
D. Explainability and NLP-Based Severity Scoring.
Their information is represented in the form of sentences in drug interaction databases such as DrugBank, for instance, the co-administration of these drugs may increase the risk of QT prolongation, which may lead to potentially life-threatening arrhythmias. PolyGuard automatically identifies these sentences using the Natural Language Processing engine and gives them risk point scores.
The Natural Language Processing engine works by searching for indicators that indicate the presence of specific medical keywords. The engine uses a set of 310 key medical keywords with a severity score and overlaid on an organ system. For instance, the keywords "fatal" or "cardiac arrest" would be given a very high score (90-100 points). The keywords "hepatotoxicity" or "QT prolongation" would be given an intermediate score (50-70 points). The keywords "nausea" would be given a low score (10-25 points). Another trick that the engine uses is the search for negation, such that if the word "does not cause hepatotoxicity" is in the text, then the word would be given the keyword and 80 points would be subtracted to eliminate false positives.
Each of the risk scores generated by the system has a description in simple terms, which includes the body part at risk, symptoms to be looked out for, and other medications that may be relatively safer. This is a form of accountability that is required in clinical trust and adoption [13].
V. System Design
PolyGuard is a method of entering the medication list of a patient and then processing it through seven steps, which transforms the list of raw drug names into the full report of clinical risk. There is a specific task in each step.
Step 1 - Drug Input: Either the clinician will type the medication list of the patients or she will snap a photo of the prescription label. A photo is read by an optical character recognition (OCR) system which automatically extracts the names of the drugs. All names will be then compared against the database of over 50,000 drug names to correct the spelling and format.
Step 2 Interaction Lookup The system will list all possible combinations of drugs and search each combination on DrugBank, where the written description of a known interaction (or lack thereof) will be retrieved.
Stage 3 NLP Severity Scoring The NLP engine assigns a score to each description of interaction in terms of numerical severity based on the 310-keyword clinical vocabulary in the description above.
Stage 4 - Organ Risk Mapping: Each activity is scored for the organ systems they affect to produce a risk profile of each drug pair of ten numbers, which is the risk of that drug pair to each organ system.
Stage 5 - Patient Adjustment: The scores of the organ risks are multiplied by the lab results and health conditions of the patient, yielding individual scores that are valid for this particular patient.
Stage 6-- Cascade Check: This is the sum of all individual organ scores for all drug pairs, per organ. A cascade alert is generated if the sum of any organ crosses the threshold level.
Stage 7 – Report generation: The software has the capacity to generate a clinical report in a structured format that contains a visual risk dashboard, a summary of each of the risks in layman terms, a list of symptoms to monitor, recommendations for monitoring certain organs, and safer drug habits.
VI. Solution Methodologies
A. Drug Identification and OCR.
The task of manually entering a list of drugs is time-consuming and error-prone, especially in an environment that is fast-paced like a healthcare setting. PolyGuard will be able to handle photo-based input: a doctor will take a photo of a prescription label or a bottle of used medication, and an OCR model will identify the naming of the drugs based on the photo provided. The corresponding identification of these names to the standardized database will be done using the fuzzy matching approach, which implies that spelling variations or abbreviations will also be considered.
B. Integration with DrugBank
DrugBank is an FDA-approved database of drugs and one of the largest international databases providing information on more than 14,000 drugs and 2 million drug interactions. Each record of a drug interaction includes a description of the interaction between two drugs assorted, the severity of the event, and the biological pathway. PolyGuard will also be integrated with DrugBank to offer this information for each drug pair in the treatment plan of a patient so that all future risk assessments are based on this.

C. NLP Severity Scoring - How it Works in a Nutshell.
Let’s assume that the NLP engine is a super intelligent reader who has a good knowledge of 310 medically significant words, having memorized them and being cognizant of the possible danger of each word. On the sentence such as "This combination may cause fatal cardiac arrhythmia," it identifies the word "fatal" (severe, very high, cardiovascular system) and the word "arrhythmia" (high, severe, cardiovascular system), and then sums the scores, noting that this drug combination is very dangerous to the cardiovascular system. When the sentence was changed to read that this combination does not normally cause arrhythmia, since the engine is cognizant of the word "not," the word "arrhythmia" would not be identified as a false positive, and the score would be adjusted by 80 percent. The end result of this process would be a series of numerical scores, one for each drug pair, reflecting the strength of each coordination in an equal and uniform manner.
D. Patient Vulnerability Scoring- Lean Terms in Simple Speak.
The drug interaction data has a natural baseline risk score for each organ system. These baselines are then adjusted depending on the healthiness of that same specific organ in this patient. This is simply a multiplication of the adjustment: if a patient has limited functionality in his or her kidneys (as evidenced by a low value of eGFR), the adjustment score must be multiplied higher - perhaps by 2.0 or 2.5 - to indicate that a drug interaction with the kidneys is much more risky to the kidneys than to a patient with healthy kidneys. This can similarly be done for the healthiness of the liver (as evidenced by the value of ALT and AST) and the healthiness of blood clotting (as evidenced by platelet counts and INR) and heart health (as evidenced by vital signs and history of diagnosis). Patients who have normal values have multipliers that are close to 1.0 hence their scores are not altered much.
E. Cascade Detection Mechanism
Once the calculation of patient-adjusted scores is completed for all pairs of drugs, the cascade module asks a question that is very simple yet very important for each of the ten organ systems: What is the total burden of this organ of all the drugs together? It adds up the scores of all pairs of drugs that affect the kidneys, the liver score of all pairs that affect the liver, and so on. The system initiates a cascade alert if the running total of any organ exceeds a danger level set. This is very important as one particular pair may not have caused an alert, but the total burden of the whole treatment regimen can be dangerous, especially when it comes to patients who have the organs that have already been affected.

VII. Design Rationale and Expected System Behaviour
Polyguard is a system that has not yet been put into place, therefore, the reasoning behind each decision will be explained in this section and how the system will work once it is placed and tested.
A. An explanation of why Organ-Level Scoring Will Enhance Clinical Usefulness.
A general severity level of Low, Moderate, and High does not give the clinician any direction. But if the clinician understands that a combination of medications is extremely dangerous for the cardiovascular system, in this case, the clinician will understand that they need to order an ECG and monitor the blood pressure. They will understand how to interpret the creatinine levels in order to adjust the dosage if they understand that the danger is to the kidneys. Organ-level scoring takes this very general message and turns it into a specific directive. The more specific the message, the better the chance that it will be acted upon rather than ignored.
B. The explanation for how Patient Adjustment Will reduce Alert Fatigue.
One of the biggest challenges in clinical informatics is alert fatigue, which refers to the phenomenon of healthcare professionals ignoring warnings issued to them about a possible safety problem because of the volume of warnings they receive. This is mainly because all patients are now receiving the same warning notice irrespective of whether they are at risk or not. PolyGuard will not issue more warnings to high-risk patients than are required by providing different levels of warnings depending on the health data of individual patients, and the number of high-level warnings will be more valuable. A healthy patient who is mimicking the functioning of organs well and does not have comorbidities will trigger very little warning. A particular warning will trigger a particular warning in a patient who is currently taking six medications and has kidney problems, diabetes, and heart failure. This particular warning will trigger fewer warnings of high severity, and those that trigger should be more likely to be attended to.
C. Why Cascade Detection Fills a Clinical Problem missing the solution.
The biggest blind spot in pairwise drug interaction screening is that it cannot see the forest for the trees. Patients are isolated with each of the two drugs, and patients do not isolate drugs. There are fifteen potential drug pairs of the patient who is under six medications. In the case where both pairs have moderately risky kidneys, a pair system will not be alarming. However, when fifteen pairs all affect the kidney, the cumulative effect is tremendous. Cascade detection closes this gap, as it takes into account the whole burden of organs, and not one pair in particular. This has a lot of relevance, especially in the elderly and in patients who harbor other chronic diseases - this happens to be the patients who are at their weakest and most vulnerable to fall prey to complex drug regimens.
D. The reason why Rule Based NLP is appropriate for this task.
There are two main types of NLP approaches: rule-based systems, where the rules are set by a human expert, and machine learning systems, where the rules are determined from experience. Rule-based NLP is suitable for a clinical safety system. All decisions can be traced back and explained: "if the system has determined that a given pair of drugs is at high risk of cardiovascular events, it should be able to demonstrate how and what keywords were used to arrive at the scoring and why." This is not only a good choice, but it is also a requirement in the medical field and is legal [13]. It would be very difficult to defend a machine learning system that makes the correct predictions but does not have a good reason for doing so in the medical field.

VIII. Conclusion and Future Work
PolyGuard, a specific personalized AI-driven Drug-Drug Interaction Risk Assessment System, proposed in this paper, fills in three specific gaps in the current state-of-the-art clinical practice: the absence of organ-specific risk information, the absence of the capability to incorporate the individual health status of patients, and the absence of the capability to identify multi-drug toxicity throughout the entire treatment period.
PolyGuard is an infrastructure that employs a seven-step pipeline to process a list of drugs input by a patient and generate a risk report that is easily interpretable, such as explainable and at the organ level, personalized, as the output. Its three algorithmic components: multi-organ risk decomposition, patient-specific vulnerability scoring, and polypharmacy cascade detection, each attempt to fill in a specific, identified gap in the current systems. The text is translated with drug interaction database to generate structured risk scores to be automated by the NLP engine; similarly, the system architecture is rule-based so that all results can be explained and justified to a clinician.
Future work would involve the following six directions: (i) the development of the full system with the integration of the DrugBank API and the clinical interface, (ii) the empirical evaluation of the system on labeled drug interaction datasets with expert labels for the organs and severity of interactions, (iii) the addition of pharmacogenomic components (such as the CYP450 enzyme variants), in an attempt to capture the individual drug metabolism profiles of patients, (iv) the extension of the system to include herbal supplements, over-the-counter drugs, and nutraceuticals, (v) the development of a federated learning module to enable the system to be trained based on real-world clinical outcomes,
